# HELPER -> Trust Engine / FARANIK -> Commercial Engine

Date: `2026-04-12`
Status: `strategic execution memo`

## Purpose

This document defines a concrete dual-track strategy for:

- `HELPER` as the public trust engine
- `FARANIK` as the semi-closed commercial engine

It also provides:

- a probability matrix for investor and buyer outcomes
- a 90-day execution order
- success criteria
- failure modes that would weaken the strategy

## Core Thesis

The strategy works only if the market reads both projects as one coherent progression:

1. `HELPER` proves execution quality, technical honesty, governance discipline, and public trustworthiness.
2. `FARANIK` converts that trust into a higher-upside commercial narrative with a more defensible product surface.

If the market instead reads the two projects as disconnected efforts, the strategy weakens. In that failure mode:

- `HELPER` looks like a polished showcase
- `FARANIK` looks like another early concept
- the founder signal becomes `breadth without conversion`

The strategic requirement is therefore strict:

`FARANIK must be framed as the next commercial layer built on the trust earned by HELPER, not as a separate moonshot.`

## Strategic Roles

### HELPER: Trust Engine

HELPER should do four jobs:

1. prove the founder can finish hard engineering work
2. prove claims are bounded and auditable
3. create a public entry point for design partners and technical evaluators
4. reduce skepticism before FARANIK is shown

### FARANIK: Commercial Engine

FARANIK should do four jobs:

1. carry the higher-value product narrative
2. create a clearer monetizable wedge
3. preserve a semi-closed moat where openness would weaken advantage
4. convert technical trust into buyer or investor conversations

## Non-Negotiable Positioning Rule

The market-facing story must be:

`HELPER proved that the team can build and govern a real local-first AI operator system. FARANIK is the next system layer that turns those capabilities into a more valuable research-and-autonomy product with controlled access.`

The market-facing story must not be:

`Here are two separate ambitious AI projects.`

## Why Probability Improves

The increase does not come from having two repositories. It comes from combining:

- public proof of execution from `HELPER`
- reduced trust friction for new conversations
- a more commercially valuable and still defensible surface in `FARANIK`
- a believable founder narrative of progression rather than drift

## The Actual Transfer Mechanism From HELPER To FARANIK

Trust from HELPER can transfer through:

- execution credibility
- engineering rigor
- governance maturity
- honest claims discipline
- public technical visibility

Trust from HELPER does not automatically transfer through:

- product-market fit
- willingness to pay
- enterprise demand
- strategic defensibility

That means `HELPER` lowers skepticism, but `FARANIK` still has to earn commercial legitimacy.

## 90-Day Strategy

## Day 1-30: Lock The Narrative And Build The Trust Surface

### Primary Objective

Turn `HELPER` into a clean public proof surface and define `FARANIK` as the logical next commercial step.

### HELPER Actions

1. Freeze the public story.
   Result:
   one concise public narrative across `README`, deck, docs, and outreach.

2. Pick one ICP for HELPER.
   Recommended operating assumption:
   `teams that need local-first operator workflows for research, planning, and controlled generation`.

3. Publish one canonical ROI-shaped use case.
   Result:
   one short, measurable workflow proof instead of broad platform language.

4. Build a public evaluator intake path.
   Result:
   demo request, technical reviewer intake, design partner intake.

5. Start external signal collection.
   Result:
   target `10` credible external technical viewers and `3` serious feedback loops.

### FARANIK Actions

1. Define the commercial wedge in one sentence.
   Working example:
   `FARANIK is a controlled research-and-world-modeling system for evidence-backed discovery and safe autonomy workflows.`

2. Define what stays open and what stays semi-closed.
   Open:
   architecture principles, selected docs, public-safe proof materials.
   Semi-closed:
   premium workflows, internal eval assets, enterprise deployment details, privileged runtime artifacts.

3. Create a commercial memo before heavy implementation.
   Result:
   one document answering:
   who pays, why now, why this is not a commodity wrapper, what is the moat.

4. Make the project git-ready and investor-review-ready even if it is not yet published.
   Result:
   clean repository history, readable charter, roadmap, security boundary, packaging posture.

### Day 1-30 Success Criteria

- HELPER has one sharp public narrative
- HELPER has one measurable public use case
- HELPER has an evaluator funnel
- FARANIK has one clear wedge and one commercial memo
- the bridge story `HELPER -> FARANIK` is documented explicitly

## Day 31-60: Convert Trust Into Qualified Conversations

### Primary Objective

Use HELPER to start conversations, but steer the highest-value conversations toward FARANIK.

### HELPER Actions

1. Run founder-led demos.
   Target:
   `8-12` live demos with technical users, operators, founders, or potential design partners.

2. Capture public-safe proof artifacts from real sessions.
   Result:
   short demo clips, before/after workflow proofs, operator stories.

3. Publish a compact technical deep dive.
   Result:
   one article or memo that explains why HELPER is structurally different from generic agent wrappers.

4. Open a controlled feedback loop.
   Result:
   track repeat objections, repeat praise, and repeated requests.

### FARANIK Actions

1. Build only the minimum believable kernel.
   Required target:
   one coherent narrow loop proving:
   observation -> memory -> hypothesis -> evaluation -> safe promotion boundary

2. Do not build breadth first.
   Explicitly avoid:
   broad autonomous claims, hardware theatre, and loose AGI messaging.

3. Create a private alpha story.
   Result:
   selected reviewers can see architecture, bounded demos, and a crisp roadmap without getting the full engine.

4. Start design partner discovery around the wedge.
   Target:
   `3-5` conversations specifically about the commercial problem, not about general AI philosophy.

### Day 31-60 Success Criteria

- HELPER generates repeatable inbound interest
- at least `3` conversations explicitly transition from HELPER trust into FARANIK curiosity
- FARANIK has a minimal believable kernel
- at least `2` design-partner candidates exist

## Day 61-90: Launch FARANIK In Semi-Closed Mode

### Primary Objective

Use the trust earned by HELPER to release FARANIK in a way that is visible enough to attract serious interest but closed enough to preserve strategic value.

### HELPER Actions

1. Keep HELPER stable and honest.
   Do not overload it with new experimental scope.

2. Use HELPER as the public proof-of-discipline surface.
   Result:
   governance, proof paths, technical narrative, and credibility remain clean.

3. Route serious commercial conversations away from feature requests for HELPER and toward FARANIK partner access.

### FARANIK Actions

1. Publish in semi-closed mode.
   Recommended shape:
   a public landing surface plus controlled private alpha access.

2. Show enough to trigger serious dialogue.
   Show:
   mission, architecture, boundaries, selected demo artifacts, evaluation posture, roadmap.
   Hold back:
   high-value private runtime assets, sensitive eval bundles, premium workflow details, enterprise deployment details.

3. Create the partner offer.
   Offer shape:
   `private alpha`, `design partner track`, `technical diligence session`, `pilot discussion`.

4. Prepare the investor/buyer room.
   Required materials:
   narrative memo, technical memo, roadmap, proof bundle, moat memo, team/risk memo.

### Day 61-90 Success Criteria

- FARANIK has a semi-closed market surface
- at least `2` serious design-partner conversations are active
- at least `1` investor or strategic conversation references both HELPER and FARANIK together
- the market reads FARANIK as the commercial extension, not as a disconnected project

## Decision Rules

### Double Down On The Strategy If

- HELPER creates trust without scope confusion
- FARANIK gets repeated commercial curiosity from people who first saw HELPER
- the same wedge keeps coming up in partner conversations

### Correct The Strategy If

- HELPER attracts only hobbyist attention
- FARANIK conversations stay philosophical instead of commercial
- people do not understand why both projects exist

### Stop And Reframe If

- the market reads the two projects as founder distraction
- HELPER requires too much maintenance to remain credible
- FARANIK has no believable near-term wedge beyond long-horizon AGI rhetoric

## Main Risks

1. Focus dilution
   If both projects advance too broadly, neither converts into market trust.

2. Narrative drift
   If HELPER and FARANIK are described with different buyer stories, the trust transfer weakens.

3. Premature secrecy
   If FARANIK is too hidden, it cannot benefit from HELPER trust.

4. Premature openness
   If FARANIK is opened too widely, the moat may weaken before commercial leverage appears.

5. Engineering overproduction
   If too much is built before partner learning, product-market feedback arrives too late.

## Recommended Strategic Posture

The recommended posture for the next `90` days is:

- `HELPER` stays public, stable, honest, and proof-heavy
- `FARANIK` stays focused, narrow, commercial, and selectively visible
- all external communication frames `FARANIK` as the next value layer made credible by `HELPER`

## Final Operating Conclusion

Opening `HELPER` and executing the strongest trust-building actions can materially increase the probability of attracting an investor or buyer.

That probability increases further if `FARANIK` is built and launched as a semi-closed commercial engine that clearly inherits trust from `HELPER`.

The strategy fails if the market sees two unrelated ambitious projects.

The strategy strengthens if the market sees one progression:

`public trust engine -> controlled commercial engine -> design partners -> investor or buyer process`
